## THE iRxREMINDER BRANDSCRIPT

Before rewriting any copy, every word on the site should flow from this BrandScript. This is the single source of truth for all messaging.

### 1. CHARACTER (The Hero = Your Customer)
The hero is NOT iRxReminder. The hero is the healthcare professional who is responsible for patient outcomes.

**Primary heroes (in priority order):**
- Clinical researcher running a medication study who needs participants to actually take their meds
- Behavioral health director managing patients transitioning from inpatient to community living
- Pharmacy director trying to differentiate and retain customers
- Health system administrator trying to reduce readmissions and increase bundled payment margins

**What the hero wants (one clear thing):**
Confidence that their patients are taking medications correctly — without adding burden to their staff.

### 2. PROBLEM

**External problem (the surface-level, tangible issue):**
Patients don't take their medications as prescribed. Studies lose participants. Behavioral health clients destabilize. Pharmacies can't track what happens after the patient leaves.

**Internal problem (how it makes the hero FEEL):**
"I have no visibility into what's happening between visits. I'm flying blind, and when something goes wrong, it lands on me."

**Philosophical problem (the 'this shouldn't be' belief):**
We have effective medications for nearly every chronic condition. No one should lose their independence — or their life — because they forgot a pill.

**The villain (the root cause, not a person):**
The current tools are broken. Pill organizers require sorting (which causes errors and ER visits). Text reminders nag and get ignored. Wireless bottle caps count openings but can't control dosing. Every existing solution adds burden instead of removing it.

### 3. GUIDE (iRxReminder)

**Empathy statement:**
We built iRxReminder after a decade of research into why people with dementia lose their independence. We know what it's like to watch a patient decline not because the medication failed — but because the system around them did.

**Authority signals:**
- NIH/NIA funded research
- University of Michigan Cancer Center clinical study
- 12+ patents
- Published peer-reviewed data
- 10+ years of R&D
- NewChip Accelerator graduate
- Chief Medical Officer (Owen Muir, M.D.) on team
- BioEnterprise portfolio company

### 4. PLAN (3 simple steps)

**Step 1:** Schedule a pilot — We'll configure the platform for your use case (research, behavioral health, pharmacy, or health system).

**Step 2:** Deploy to patients — Pharmacy loads medications into pods. Patients use the Hold, Place, and Tilt gesture to dispense. Your team monitors everything from the Control Center.

**Step 3:** See results in real time — Every dose taken or missed is reported back immediately. Intervene only when needed. Finish studies on time. Keep patients stable.

### 5. CALL TO ACTION

**Direct CTA:** "Schedule a Pilot" or "See It In Action" (not "Learn More" — that's weak)

**Transitional CTA:** "Download the Adherence Problem Brief" or "See the Research" (for people not ready to talk to sales)

### 6. FAILURE (What happens if they don't act — use sparingly)

- 700,000 ER visits per year from medication non-adherence
- 125,000 deaths annually
- $320 billion in avoidable costs
- Research studies run over budget by 30% due to participant dropout
- Behavioral health clients destabilize, requiring hospitalization or incarceration
- Staff burnout from crisis management that could have been prevented

### 7. SUCCESS (The promised land — paint this vividly)

- Researchers finish studies on schedule and on budget with clean, real-time data
- Behavioral health agencies see patients remain stable, reducing crisis interventions and staff turnover
- Pharmacies build lasting relationships through shared adherence data and differentiated service
- Health systems reduce readmissions and increase bundled payment margins
- Patients achieve high adherence — not through nagging, but through a system that only intervenes when they actually forget

## COPY RULES — THE iRx VOICE

### What to kill:
- **"Innovative" / "cutting-edge" / "state-of-the-art" / "revolutionary"** — These are empty. Every healthcare startup says this. Replace with specifics.
- **"Leveraging" / "utilizing" / "empowering" / "synergizing"** — Corporate filler. Say what you mean plainly.
- **"Solution" as a standalone noun** — Overused to the point of meaninglessness in B2B. Say what the thing actually IS.
- **Passive voice** — "Adherence is improved by..." → "Patients take their medications on time."
- **Long compound sentences** — If a sentence has more than one comma, break it up.
- **Self-congratulatory tone** — "We're proud to announce..." → Just state the fact.
- **Starting sentences with "Our platform..."** — The hero is the customer, not you. Start with THEIR reality.

### What to write like:
- **AdhereTech's directness:** "Because every dose — and every day — counts." Simple. Human. Clear.
- **Syntra's confidence:** "Physician-level reasoning." Bold claim, immediately backed with a demo. No hedging.
- **Wellth's outcome focus:** "Better outcomes with behavioral economics." The headline IS the value prop.
- **Omada's specificity:** "$2,540 cost savings per member at year 2." Numbers, not adjectives.

### Voice principles:
1. **Lead with the customer's pain, not your product.** Every section starts with their world, not yours.
2. **Specifics over superlatives.** "12 patents" beats "innovative technology." "90-day supply per pod" beats "long-lasting."
3. **Short sentences signal confidence.** Long sentences signal uncertainty. When in doubt, cut it shorter.
4. **One idea per section.** If you can't summarize a section in one sentence, it's trying to say too much.
5. **Write at an 8th grade reading level.** Not because buyers are unsophisticated — because busy people skim, and clarity always wins.

## PAGE-BY-PAGE COPY DIRECTION

### HOMEPAGE

**Current problem:** Likely reads like a product brochure. Talks about iRx first, customer second.

**StoryBrand homepage structure (in this exact order):**

**Section 1: Hero (above the fold)**
- Headline: State what the hero gets. Not what you are.
- BAD: "The Intelligent Medication Adherence Platform"
- BAD: "Empowering Patients and Healthcare Professionals"
- GOOD: "Know exactly when your patients take their medications. Intervene when they don't."
- ALTERNATIVE: "Medication adherence you can see, measure, and act on — in real time."
- Sub-headline: One sentence clarifying how. "iRxReminder connects healthcare teams to their patients' daily medication behavior through smart dispensing, real-time monitoring, and targeted intervention."
- CTA: "Schedule a Pilot" (primary, bold) + "See the Research" (secondary, text link)
- NO paragraphs here. No company history. No mission statement.

**Section 2: The Stakes (the problem)**
- Short. 2-3 lines max + supporting stats.
- "700,000 ER visits. 125,000 deaths. $320 billion in avoidable costs. Every year. Medication non-adherence isn't a minor inconvenience — it's one of healthcare's most expensive unsolved problems."
- Then one line on WHY it's unsolved: "Current tools either nag patients into ignoring them or count bottle openings without knowing if the right dose was taken."

**Section 3: The Guide (position iRx as the answer — briefly)**
- NOT: "iRxReminder was founded in 2011 by Dr. Anthony Sterns after..."
- YES: "Built on a decade of NIH-funded research into medication behavior, iRxReminder is the only platform that controls dispensing, monitors adherence, and connects the healthcare team — all in real time."
- Trust signals: logo row of University of Michigan, NIH/NIA, Summa Health, Kent State, NewChip, OCEAN, BioEnterprise. No explanations needed — logos do the work.

**Section 4: The Plan (3-step visual)**
- "How It Works"
- Step 1: "Configure" — Set up regimens, schedules, and patient education in the Control Center.
- Step 2: "Deploy" — Patients use the iLidRx pod and iRxCapture app. No sorting. No nagging. Just smart, gentle alerts when they actually forget.
- Step 3: "Monitor" — Every dose is tracked in real time. See who's on track, who needs help, and intervene before a crisis — not after.

**Section 5: Product showcase (visual, not text-heavy)**
- Show the three components: Pod, App, Control Center
- One line each. Let the visuals carry this section.
- "See the full platform →" link to Platform page

**Section 6: Who it's for (solution areas — brief)**
- 3-4 cards: Clinical Research / Behavioral Health / Pharmacies / Health Systems
- Each card: one sentence of the hero's problem + one sentence of the outcome
- Example for Research: "Participant dropout costs studies 30% more than planned. iRxReminder gives researchers real-time compliance data — so you finish on schedule with clean data."
- Click through to detailed Solutions page

**Section 7: Evidence (social proof)**
- Key stat: "Participants using iRxReminder achieved high adherence — even those with cognitive impairments." (from actual study data)
- 1-2 testimonials if available
- "Read the evidence →"

**Section 8: Stakes reminder (light touch — the "salt")**
- "The population of older adults is doubling by 2030. The adherence problem isn't going away. The question is whether your organization has the tools to manage it."
- CTA: "Schedule a Pilot"

**Section 9: Final CTA block**
- Dark background. Large text.
- "Ready to see what real-time medication monitoring looks like?"
- "Schedule a Pilot" button
- Below: "Or download our research brief for your team."

### PLATFORM PAGE

**Current problem:** Likely organized around product components (Pod → App → Control Center). This is inside-out thinking.

**Rewrite principle:** Organize around what the customer needs to accomplish, not what you built.

**New structure:**

**Hero:** "One platform. Three components. Complete visibility into patient medication behavior."

**Then, for EACH component (Pod, App, Control Center), use this pattern:**

1. **Start with the problem it solves** — not what it is
   - Pod: "Pill organizers cause sorting errors. Bottle caps count openings but can't verify dosing. The iLidRx pod dispenses the right medication, at the right time, one dose at a time."
   - App: "Most reminders nag. Patients tune them out within weeks. iRxCapture only alerts when a patient actually forgets — turning reminders from annoying to meaningful."
   - Control Center: "You can't manage what you can't see. The iRxControl Center shows every dose — taken or missed — across your entire patient or participant population, in real time."

2. **Then the key specs** (expandable — not on the surface)
   - Pod: 90-day supply, gesture-based dispensing, inductive charging, child-resistant, tamper-evident
   - App: auto-loads regimens from Control Center, symptom reporting, patient education delivery
   - Control Center: role-based access, calendar drill-down, real-time alerts, customizable schedules

3. **End with the outcome**
   - Pod: "Patients take the right dose. Period."
   - App: "Adherence through support, not nagging."
   - Control Center: "Intervene early. Not after the ER visit."

### SOLUTIONS PAGE

**Rewrite principle:** Each solution section starts with the hero's world, not iRx's capabilities.

**For each audience, use this pattern:**

**Headline = Their goal, not your feature**
- Research: "Finish studies on time, on budget, with data you can trust"
- Behavioral Health: "Keep clients stable. Reduce crisis. Lower staff burnout."
- Pharmacies: "Differentiate with technology your competitors can't match"
- Health Systems: "Reduce readmissions. Increase bundled payment margins."

**Then 2-3 lines of their problem** — written in THEIR language:
- Research: "You've designed a clean protocol. But 30% of participants drop out unannounced. The ones who stay may not be taking their medication on schedule. You're collecting data that doesn't reflect what actually happened."
- Behavioral Health: "Your clients leave inpatient care stabilized. Within weeks, many stop taking medications. You find out when they're back in crisis — not before."

**Then the iRx answer** — 2-3 specific, concrete capabilities:
- Research: "iRxReminder tracks every dose event in real time. You see exactly who's following protocol and who isn't. Intervene with the 20% who need help instead of bothering the 80% who don't."

**Then a tangible outcome:**
- Research: "Save up to 20% of study costs by reducing participant dropout and protocol deviations."

## COPY PATTERNS STOLEN FROM COMPETITORS

### From AdhereTech (closest competitor — study closely):
- **"No setup. No patient app. Always on cellular connection."** — Three short fragments. Each kills an objection. This is elite B2B copy. iRx should have similar objection-killing fragments for each audience.
- **"Aidia lifts patient retention by over 50% in 1st year across disease states."** — Specific. Measurable. One sentence. iRx needs equivalents.
- **"Patient satisfaction scores consistently exceed 95%."** — Let data speak. No adjectives needed.

### From Syntra:
- **Section headers that are benefits, not categories.** "Intelligence for Outcomes" not "Platform Capabilities." "Works with your Workflow" not "Integrations."
- **Sub-sections with verb-driven labels:** "Revenue Discovery," "Outpatient Coding." Not "Feature 1," "Feature 2."

### From Wellth:
- **Patient quotes as social proof.** "My health has gotten a lot better since I started the Wellth program." Real voice, not polished corporate testimonial. If iRx has any patient or researcher quotes, use them exactly as spoken.

### From Omada Health:
- **ROI specificity.** "$2,540 cost savings per member at year 2." If iRx can quantify the "save up to 20% of study costs" claim more precisely, do it.
- **Audience segmentation in the CTA:** Omada asks visitors to identify their employer/plan first. iRx could do: "I'm a researcher / I run a behavioral health agency / I manage a pharmacy / I lead a health system."

## GLOBAL COPY CHECKLIST FOR CLAUDE CODE

Before writing or rewriting ANY section of copy, run it through these filters:

- [ ] Does it start with the customer's reality, not iRx's product?
- [ ] Could a competitor copy-paste this headline and it would still make sense? If yes, it's not specific enough.
- [ ] Is there a concrete number, timeframe, or outcome? If not, add one.
- [ ] Is the CTA specific and action-oriented? ("Schedule a Pilot" not "Learn More")
- [ ] Can a non-healthcare person understand it on first read?
- [ ] Does it pass the "so what" test? After every sentence, ask "so what?" — if there's no answer, cut it.
- [ ] Is it under 20 words per sentence on average?
- [ ] Are you using "you/your" more than "we/our"?
- [ ] Does the page follow StoryBrand order? (Hero want → Problem → Guide → Plan → CTA → Stakes → Success)
- [ ] Would you say this in a conversation, or only in a brochure? If only a brochure, rewrite it.
